# Narrative frameworks

Every deck that works is built on a narrative frame. The frame is
invisible to the audience but invisible-load-bearing for the
presenter. Pick one early — usually in `design.md` — and let it
shape the slide list. Mixing frames mid-deck is the #1 cause of
"this deck doesn't land."

Below: 8 frameworks, when each fits, and the slide-skeleton each
produces. Use the closest fit; combine only when you understand
why.

---

## 1. Minto Pyramid Principle

**Use when:** Internal decks, exec updates, consulting deliverables,
anywhere the audience needs the answer FIRST and the supporting
evidence after.

**Shape:**
1. The answer / recommendation (1 slide, 1 sentence)
2. Three reasons supporting it (3 slides, one each)
3. Evidence supporting each reason (1-3 slides per reason)
4. Recap of the answer (1 slide)

**Why it works:** Decision-makers want the conclusion. Detail
exists to defend it, not to delay it. Hostile to "story arcs" —
no suspense, no buildup. That's the point.

**Don't use when:** Selling something, telling a story, motivating
change. Minto answers questions; it doesn't move people.

---

## 2. SCQA — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer

**Use when:** Strategy proposals, change-management decks, "why we
need to do this differently" arguments. Distills Minto's setup
into a tighter open.

**Shape:**
1. **Situation** — what the audience already knows + agrees with
   (1-2 slides). Establishes shared ground.
2. **Complication** — what changed / what's broken (2-3 slides).
   The reason we're here.
3. **Question** — the question the complication forces (1 slide,
   one sentence, often unspoken but felt).
4. **Answer** — the proposal (5-10 slides) + supporting evidence.

**Why it works:** The audience builds the "what should we do?"
question in their own head before you answer it. Your answer
feels earned, not asserted.

**Don't use when:** The audience doesn't share your read of the
situation. SCQA breaks if Situation is contested.

---

## 3. Pixar Story Spine

**Use when:** Product launches, fundraising narratives, anything
emotional. Pixar's writers use this to find a story before
animating. Pitch decks that move people use it whether they
know it or not.

**Shape:**
> Once upon a time, [setup].
> Every day, [routine].
> Until one day, [inciting incident].
> Because of that, [first consequence].
> Because of that, [second consequence].
> Until finally, [climax / resolution].
> Ever since then, [new normal].

Maps to slides as a 7-section arc. Each "because of that" is its
own evidence slide.

**Why it works:** Cause and effect chain. The audience can't
look away — they have to know what happens next. Most powerful
narrative frame for fundraising decks.

**Don't use when:** Tactical decks, dashboards, anything where
"what should we do Monday morning" matters more than emotional
buy-in.

---

## 4. Nancy Duarte — Contrast Structure ("Resonate")

**Use when:** Vision decks, all-hands, change leadership talks,
TED-style keynotes. Designed specifically for talks that move
audiences to act.

**Shape:** Alternate between **what is** and **what could be**
throughout the deck. Open with what is (the dissatisfying
present). Cut to what could be (the desirable future). Back to
what is (the cost of staying). Back to what could be (the
mechanism to get there). Close with a "new bliss" — the world
after the audience takes action.

The contrast IS the engine. The deck oscillates; the audience
feels the gap; you call them to close it.

**Why it works:** The structure makes the call-to-action feel
inevitable. Duarte analyzed great speeches (MLK, Jobs, Reagan)
and found this pattern is what they all share.

**Don't use when:** The audience isn't ready to act. Contrast
without action is just complaining.

## 5. Guy Kawasaki 10/20/30 — Investor Pitch

**Use when:** Pitching investors. Period. Don't use for other
contexts.

**Shape:** Exactly 10 slides, exactly 20 minutes, font no
smaller than 30pt. The 10 slides:

1. Title
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Business model
5. Underlying magic / technology
6. Marketing & sales plan
7. Competition
8. Team
9. Projections + milestones
10. Status + call to action

**Why it works:** Investors have seen 1000 decks this quarter.
Predictable structure means they can listen to YOU instead of
hunting for the slide they need. 30pt font means you can't hide
behind text.

**Don't use when:** Anything other than a fundraising pitch. The
structure is overfit to "convince a stranger to write a check."

---

## 6. Sequoia Capital Pitch Template

**Use when:** Same as Kawasaki — fundraising — but with VCs who
specifically use the Sequoia template (most US tech VCs do).

**Shape:** Company purpose / Problem / Solution / Why now /
Market size / Competition / Product / Business model / Team /
Financials.

Roughly equivalent to Kawasaki's 10 but with explicit "Why now"
and "Market size" slides upfront. "Why now" is the slide that
gets rejected pitches rejected — if you can't answer why the
opportunity exists today and not 3 years ago, investors won't bet.

**Don't use when:** Anything non-fundraising.

---

## 7. Steve Jobs Keynote Structure

**Use when:** Product launches, demo-driven talks, anything
where you're unveiling something. Most-copied keynote pattern;
copy with care because the copies usually feel hollow.

**Shape:**
1. **The headline** — one sentence that frames everything
   ("Today Apple reinvents the phone.")
2. **Establish the problem** — show the dissatisfying status quo.
   Often with a competitor screenshot.
3. **"There are three things we want to show you today."** Rule of
   3. Set expectations.
4. **Each of the three, in turn**, with demos.
5. **"And one more thing…"** — the surprise.
6. **Pricing & availability.**

**Why it works:** Tension and release. The audience feels the
problem, then watches the solution land. The "one more thing"
exploits the peak-end rule — people remember the highest
emotional moment and the ending.

**Don't use when:** No product to demo. Or no actual surprise —
"one more thing" without a real surprise is condescending.

---

## 8. Beyond Bullet Points (Cliff Atkinson) — 5-Act Tech Talk

**Use when:** Technical conference talks, internal architecture
reviews, postmortems. Designed for "explain a complex thing in
30-45 minutes" contexts.

**Shape (5 acts, ~3-5 slides each):**

1. **Setting** — where we are, what the system does
2. **Role** — what role the audience plays / what they care about
3. **Point of view** — the speaker's claim or thesis
4. **Inciting incident** — what happened / what we learned
5. **Resolution** — how it ended / what to do with the lesson

**Why it works:** Treats the technical talk as a story instead
of a structure. The audience remembers the narrative; the
details land because they're hooked on a frame.

**Don't use when:** Tutorial / workshop format where the audience
follows along step-by-step. Tutorials are linear, not narrative.

---

## 9. TED Talk Arc

**Use when:** Idea-driven keynote, conference plenary, "spread
a worthy idea". 18 minutes max.

**Shape:**
1. **Hook** — a moment that grabs attention (story, surprise,
   provocative question). First 60 seconds.
2. **The idea** — one core idea, stated explicitly.
3. **The body** — why the idea is true / useful / non-obvious.
   3-5 supporting beats.
4. **The application** — what the audience does with the idea.
5. **The closing image** — a metaphor or story that anchors the
   idea in memory.

**Why it works:** TED's research-backed format. The hook + the
single-idea constraint forces ruthless editing.

**Don't use when:** You have more than one idea. Splitting an
idea is hard but necessary — TED talks fail when they try to
cover two ideas in 18 minutes.

## Picking a framework

| Audience / context                           | Frame                              |
| -------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Board / exec update                          | Minto                              |
| Strategy proposal                            | SCQA                               |
| Investor pitch                               | Kawasaki 10/20/30 or Sequoia       |
| Product launch                               | Steve Jobs structure               |
| Conference tech talk                         | Beyond Bullet Points 5-act         |
| Conference idea talk                         | TED arc                            |
| Vision / change leadership                   | Duarte contrast                    |
| Fundraising / emotional buy-in               | Pixar story spine                  |
| Sales pitch (1:1 or small group)             | SCQA (open) → Jobs structure (close)|

Combine sparingly. A common useful combo: open with SCQA to
establish "we need to do something," then use the Jobs structure
to unveil the something.
