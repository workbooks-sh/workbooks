# Narrative frameworks for short video

Short video doesn't have time for a 3-act setup with subplots. The
frame has to land in 5 seconds or it's lost. These five frameworks
are the ones that actually work under that constraint. Pick one
early — usually in `design.md` — and let it shape the shot list.

---

## 1. Hook–Promise–Payoff

**Use when:** Social ads, vertical short-form, anything where
attention is the bottleneck (TikTok, Reels, Shorts).

**Shape (typically 15–30s):**
1. **Hook (0–2s)** — visual or claim that interrupts. "This
   killed my SaaS." A close-up of a broken thing. A number on a
   black card.
2. **Promise (2–5s)** — what you're about to show. Implicit
   contract with the viewer.
3. **Payoff (5–25s)** — deliver. Faster cuts, single thread.
4. **Tag (25–30s)** — brand mark + call. Single line.

**Why it works:** Vertical-video attention curves drop hard at 3s
and again at 8s. The Hook–Promise structure crosses both with a
reason to keep watching, not a slow build.

**Don't use when:** The audience already chose to watch (a logged-in
product tour, a webinar replay). Skipping the hook is fine; you
have their attention.

---

## 2. Problem–Solution (the classic explainer)

**Use when:** Product explainers, B2B SaaS demos, "what is X" videos.

**Shape (typically 60–90s):**
1. **The problem (0–15s)** — concrete, named, painful. NOT "managing
   data is hard." DO "your team rebuilt the same dashboard four times
   this quarter."
2. **Why it persists (15–25s)** — one sentence on what existing
   solutions miss.
3. **The shift (25–35s)** — the new lens / approach. "What if your
   dashboard was a workbook?"
4. **How it works (35–70s)** — the actual product. Show, don't
   tell. Use real screens.
5. **Outcome (70–85s)** — concrete win. Customer quote, metric.
6. **Tag + CTA (85–90s)** — one URL, one verb.

**Why it works:** Mirrors how prospects actually search. They name
their pain, then they search for relief. The framework lets you
meet them at the exact phrase they'd type.

**Don't use when:** Brand videos, recruiting videos, anything where
the goal is identity not adoption.

---

## 3. Three-Act (mini-feature)

**Use when:** Brand films, product launch films, founder videos —
anything over 90s where you need emotional buildup.

**Shape (typically 90s–3min):**
1. **Act I — Setup (first 25%)** — establish protagonist + world.
2. **Plot point 1** — the disruption. The thing that breaks the
   status quo.
3. **Act II — Confrontation (middle 50%)** — protagonist tries,
   adapts, struggles.
4. **Plot point 2** — the realization / pivot.
5. **Act III — Resolution (final 25%)** — the new normal. Tag.

**Why it works:** Audience has narrative literacy from a lifetime
of movies. Hitting the beats triggers emotional expectations the
audience can't NOT respond to. Don't underuse this on a 15-second
ad; it needs runway.

**Don't use when:** You don't have 90 seconds. Three-act below
that timescale just feels truncated.

---

## 4. Before / After (the demo)

**Use when:** Product demos, screen recordings, tools that have
a clear "ugh, this is what I do today" / "wait, that's it?"
swing.

**Shape (typically 30–60s):**
1. **Before (0–15s)** — current workflow. Show the friction,
   don't narrate it. Mouse hunting through menus, switching apps,
   manual copy-paste. Real-time, no speedup.
2. **Hand-off frame (~15s)** — one visual beat that says "now
   watch this."
3. **After (15–55s)** — same task with the product. Compress where
   real-time would be slow, but never speed up the part that's
   the actual surprise.
4. **Tag (55–60s)** — same brand mark, one URL.

**Why it works:** Comparison is the unit of demo. Audiences don't
register "feature X exists" — they register "the way I do this
now is bad and there's something better."

**Don't use when:** The product's value isn't a single-task swap
(e.g. brand-positioning workbooks, identity tools, ambient
analytics — show those differently).

---

## 5. Single-Statement Manifesto

**Use when:** Brand films, recruiting, launch promos with a
worldview to plant. Apple's "Think Different." Nike's "Just Do It."

**Shape (typically 30–60s):**
1. **The statement (0–5s)** — one line on a clean frame. Read it
   slowly. Hold the silence.
2. **The case (5–45s)** — montage proving the line. One beat per
   shot, no narration cutting against visuals.
3. **The repeat (45–55s)** — the statement again, this time
   landing differently because of the montage.
4. **Tag (55–60s)** — brand mark. Often no URL.

**Why it works:** Builds identity, not adoption. Skips the
"problem" step entirely because the statement IS the problem (a
statement of what the audience already feels). Designed to be
watched twice.

**Don't use when:** You're trying to drive a click. Manifesto
videos plant an idea; product videos drive a button-press.
Different tools.

---

## Mixing frameworks

Don't. Pick one. Hooking with Problem–Solution and then
shifting to Manifesto mid-video reads as confused. If the brief
seems to need two frameworks, you actually need two videos.
