# Video types — pick the right kind before you pick the shots

Each type has a typical audience, channel, length, composition
count, narrative frame, and a list of mistakes that kill it.
Match what the user asked for to the closest type before writing
`design.md`.

Length is the load-bearing dimension. A 15-second social ad and
a 5-minute pitch video are different products built with
different vocabulary; pretending one is the other is the most
common failure mode.

---

## Sales pitch video

- **Audience:** A specific prospect or a narrow prospect segment.
  Often watched alone on a laptop, not in a room.
- **Venue / channel:** Sent as a link in a sales follow-up email,
  embedded in a pitch deck, attached to a proposal.
- **Length:** 90–180 seconds.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1–2 compositions, 8–15 shots total.
- **Frame:** SCQA open → Jobs-style close. Or Hook–Promise–Payoff
  if the prospect is cold.
- **Anchor composition:** Single `main.xml` with shots:
  prospect's problem in their words → cost of doing nothing → our
  approach → 2 case studies → next step.
- **Critical shot:** The prospect's problem in the prospect's own
  words, shot 1. If you can't open with that, the rest doesn't
  land.
- **Common failures:**
  - Generic video used for every prospect. The first 3 shots MUST
    be customised.
  - Pricing in the video. Pricing slides go in the proposal, not
    the video; the video is about value.
  - No closing CTA. End with a calendar invite or a written ask.
- **Don't use when:** the prospect already knows you. A sales pitch
  video to a warm lead reads as canned — use a 30-second update
  instead.

## Product demo

- **Audience:** Prospects evaluating the product, existing customers
  learning a new feature.
- **Venue / channel:** Product landing page, in-app onboarding,
  changelog post, sales-call attachment.
- **Length:** 60–180 seconds per feature, or 3–8 minutes for a full
  walkthrough.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition for a feature; 3–5 for a
  walkthrough (one per major flow). 6–20 shots per composition.
- **Frame:** Before–After or Problem–Solution. The viewer wants to
  see what they couldn't do, then watch you do it.
- **Anchor composition:** Heavy `<clip>` slicing of one long screen
  recording, with text overlays calling out what's happening.
- **Critical shot:** The first "do the thing" shot — within 8
  seconds of the video opening, the viewer should see the product
  doing something they can't already do.
- **Common failures:**
  - Showing setup and configuration before the payoff. Skip
    setup; assume it's done.
  - Mouse-following voiceover ("I'm clicking the menu now…").
    Captions describe intent, not mechanics.
  - No fallback for live data. Mock the data; never depend on
    the real account being in the right state at record time.
- **Don't use when:** the feature is better explained by docs.
  Demo videos age fast as UI changes; reserve them for moments
  where the demo IS the news.

## Explainer

- **Audience:** People hearing the concept for the first time.
  Often non-technical or cross-discipline.
- **Venue / channel:** Homepage hero, blog post embed, partner
  enablement, "what is this?" social posts.
- **Length:** 60–120 seconds.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition, 6–10 shots — one per
  beat of the concept.
- **Frame:** Three-Act (setup / complication / resolution) or
  Single-Statement Manifesto if the concept is one idea.
- **Anchor composition:** Heavy animated typography + simple
  shapes. The shots ARE the explanation.
- **Critical shot:** The metaphor shot — the visual that lets the
  viewer's brain map the new concept to one they already have.
- **Common failures:**
  - Two concepts in one video. Cut to one or split into two.
  - Jargon in shot 1. The audience hasn't earned the jargon yet;
    use the shot to earn it.
  - Generic stock footage as b-roll. If the explanation is
    abstract, type + shapes beat stock footage every time.
- **Don't use when:** the concept is concrete and visible (a
  product). A demo beats an explainer when there's a real thing
  to show.

## Interview / podcast clip

- **Audience:** Existing followers of the show, social audience
  discovering the clip.
- **Venue / channel:** Twitter/X, LinkedIn, YouTube Shorts,
  Instagram Reels.
- **Length:** 30–90 seconds.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition, 1–3 shots — the
  talking head, optional b-roll cut, optional title card.
- **Frame:** Pull-quote pattern — open on the quote, deliver the
  quote, close on the speaker's identity.
- **Anchor composition:** Talking-head + lower-third + burned
  captions. The audio is doing the work; the visuals support
  identity.
- **Critical shot:** The first 2 seconds — captioned and clear
  enough that a muted scroll-by reads the hook.
- **Common failures:**
  - Starting with "umm" or filler. Cut to the first complete
    thought.
  - No captions. >85% of social viewing is muted. Caption every
    word.
  - Wrong aspect ratio for the channel. Vertical for Reels /
    Shorts / TikTok; square for LinkedIn; horizontal for YouTube.
- **Don't use when:** the quote needs context the audience doesn't
  have. Clips without context land flat.

## Brand spot (60-second positioning)

- **Audience:** People who don't know the brand yet, or know it
  but haven't bought in.
- **Venue / channel:** Pre-roll on YouTube, homepage above-the-
  fold, conference-booth screen, partner-enablement deck.
- **Length:** 30–60 seconds.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition, 8–15 shots — fast
  cuts, evocative imagery, brand stamp close.
- **Frame:** Single-Statement Manifesto or Hook–Promise–Payoff.
  No teaching; pure positioning.
- **Anchor composition:** B-roll + VO + brand-mark close. Music
  is louder than usual; VO is sparse.
- **Critical shot:** The closing brand stamp — what the viewer
  remembers. Hold it 1.5–3 seconds, no decoration.
- **Common failures:**
  - Trying to explain the product. Brand spots earn the right to
    explain; they don't do the explaining.
  - Too many words. <40 words of VO for a 60-second spot.
  - Generic stock music. Sound design IS the brand here.
- **Don't use when:** the product is unknown enough that
  positioning makes no sense without explanation. Start with an
  explainer instead.

## Social ad (15–30 sec vertical / square)

- **Audience:** People scrolling, attention measured in fractions
  of a second.
- **Venue / channel:** TikTok, Reels, Shorts, paid social.
  Vertical 9:16 (1080×1920) primary; square 1:1 as fallback for
  Instagram feed.
- **Length:** 15–30 seconds. 15 is the sweet spot for paid;
  30 for organic.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition, 3–4 shots — Hook /
  Promise / Payoff / Tag.
- **Frame:** Hook–Promise–Payoff. No exceptions.
- **Anchor composition:** Vertical 9:16, hook lands ≤ 2s, every
  shot has burned captions, every shot has motion. No empty
  backgrounds.
- **Critical shot:** Shot 1 (the hook) — has to interrupt the
  scroll. If it could be a static image, it's not a hook.
- **Common failures:**
  - Slow open. >2s before the first claim = ad skipped.
  - Horizontal video shoved into vertical. Re-frame, don't pad.
  - No CTA in the tag. End with the URL and a verb.
- **Don't use when:** you need nuance. Vertical + 15s eats all
  the room nuance needs.

## Talk recap (compressed event recording)

- **Audience:** People who couldn't attend the talk, employees
  catching up, marketing distribution after the event.
- **Venue / channel:** Post-event email, conference YouTube
  channel, internal Slack drop.
- **Length:** 2–4 minutes (recap of a 30–45 min talk) or 45–90
  seconds (highlight reel).
- **Compositions / shots:** 1–2 compositions, 6–12 shots.
  Heavy `<clip>` slicing of the original talk recording.
- **Frame:** Pull-quote pattern at scale — 3–5 key claims, each
  with a 10–20 second clip of the speaker delivering it,
  intercut with the speaker's slides.
- **Anchor composition:** Speaker clip + lower-third + slide
  cutaway. Captions burned. Music low under the speaker.
- **Critical shot:** The closing one-sentence summary — usually
  the speaker's own closing line. If the speaker didn't say one,
  add a title card.
- **Common failures:**
  - Releasing the full talk instead of a recap. Different
    deliverable; release both, label them.
  - Bad audio. Talk recordings often have venue noise; pay for
    a sound pass before the cut.
  - No slide cutaways. The speaker alone on screen for 4
    minutes loses the room.
- **Don't use when:** the talk wasn't compressible. Some talks
  ARE the meandering; recapping kills them.

## Corporate / all-hands video

- **Audience:** Your company. Often distributed async to people
  who couldn't attend the live all-hands.
- **Venue / channel:** Internal email, intranet, Slack drop,
  Loom in a follow-up thread.
- **Length:** 3–8 minutes for a recap; 30–90 seconds for a
  message from leadership.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition for a short message;
  2–4 for a recap (Vision / Numbers / What's next / Asks).
- **Frame:** Minto — answer first, evidence second. Internal
  audiences want the takeaway up front.
- **Anchor composition:** Talking head + lower-third + occasional
  chart or screenshot overlay. Production restrained — over-
  produced internal video reads as PR.
- **Critical shot:** The "what you do differently next week"
  shot. Without it, the video is news, not action.
- **Common failures:**
  - Over-curated. Internal audiences smell PR copy in
    milliseconds.
  - No clear ask. Always end with "here's what you do".
  - Wrong host. Pick the person closest to the news; not
    always the CEO.
- **Don't use when:** the message is bad news that requires
  questions. Do it live; record afterward.

## Pitch video (investor / fundraising)

- **Audience:** VCs, angels, strategic acquirers. Often shared
  via warm intro; the recipient watches alone before deciding
  to take a meeting.
- **Venue / channel:** Sent as a link in a fundraising email,
  embedded in a memo, attached to a DocSend.
- **Length:** 90–180 seconds.
- **Compositions / shots:** 1 composition, 10–15 shots —
  Problem / Solution / Market / Traction / Team / Ask.
- **Frame:** Kawasaki 10/20/30 compressed to 90 seconds, or
  Sequoia template's first 7 slides.
- **Anchor composition:** Founder talking head intercut with
  product screens, trajectory chart, team faces. Closing card
  has the ask in dollars and the use-of-funds.
- **Critical shot:** The traction shot — the chart or metric
  that proves the thing works. Without it, the pitch reads as
  a deck.
- **Common failures:**
  - Skipping "why now". The most-rejected reason after team.
  - Hockey-stick projections without underlying assumptions.
    Investors mentally divide by 10.
  - Hiding the ask. State it explicitly; closing card.
- **Don't use when:** the deck already lands the pitch. Some
  rooms prefer the deck and a follow-up call over a video.

When the brief doesn't fit cleanly into a row, the brief is
underspecified — go back to the user and clarify audience or
channel before guessing.

## What this doc is NOT

A type is a *starting* shape, not a destination. Two sales pitch
videos for two different prospects should look and pace
differently — same archetype, different motion identity. The
type informs the shot list; `motion.md` + `styles.css` carry the
look. See [designing-the-look.md](designing-the-look.md).
